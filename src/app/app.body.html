In the quiet town of Marrowfield, time moved slowly — almost too slowly. The cobblestone streets echoed the same footsteps every morning, and the bell tower rang with such precision that people rarely checked their own watches. At the corner of Elm Street stood a tiny shop, known simply as Hollis & Timepieces. Inside worked an old clockmaker named Edwin Hollis, who was said to have built a clock that never needed winding.

Edwin was a quiet man with steady hands and eyes that always seemed to be measuring something invisible. He had learned his craft from his father, who had once told him that every clock had a soul — and that a true clockmaker’s job was not to build time, but to understand it. So each morning, as the sun rose through the dusty glass windows, Edwin would carefully polish gears, align springs, and whisper small apologies to the clocks that refused to tick.

One stormy evening, just as he was about to close the shop, a young woman rushed in, soaked to the bone. She carried an old, broken pocket watch and a look of quiet desperation. “It belonged to my grandfather,” she said softly. “It stopped the moment he passed.” Edwin took the watch gently, feeling its weight. The glass was cracked, the hands were frozen, and the engraving on the back had almost faded away. “I can fix it,” he said after a pause, “but it might take more than time.”
